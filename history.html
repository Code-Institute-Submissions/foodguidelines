The History of the Dietary Guidelines

400 b.c.

The Greek physician Hippocrates, the “Father of Medicine” said, “Let thy food be thy medicine and thy medicine be thy food.” This quote is from Hippocrates, considered the Father of Medicine. The Greek physician was the first to link the importance of food in the human body health.

1747

Dr. James Lind made an experiment with sailors, who generally suffer from a deadly bleeding disorder, the scurvy. The key was in their diet. The main source of nutrients was food such as bread and meat.

Dr. Lind separated three groups, one group was given salt water, other with vinegar and a third one with limes. The later were less propense to the disease, linked to lack the Vitamin C present in the fruit.

1770

Anthony Lavoisier, known as the “Father of Chemistry” discovered the concept of metabolism, which the transfer of food and oxygen into heat and water, creating energy.

1800

At the beginning of the century, scientists managed to isolate the main components of food (Carbon, Nitrogen, Hydrogen, and Oxygen), which were connected to health. Justin Liebig, a German scientist, identified the chemical structure of carbohydrates, fats, and proteins.

1897

Christiaan Eijkman was working with natives of Java who had developed the disease beriberi. Beriberi is a severe disease that causes heart disease and even paralysis.

Through his deduction, Eijkman discovered that changing the natives’ diet from white rice to brown rice cured the beriberi. It was later discovered that the husk of the unprocessed brown rice contained thiamine, or Vitamin B1.

1904

Dr. Wilbur Olin Atwater published Principles of Nutrition and Nutritive Value of Food. The main topics of the bulleting was to detail the importance of variety, proportionality and moderation. Calories measurement and an efficient, affordable diet that focused on nutrient-rich foods and less fat, sugar and starch.

1943

The Basic 7 was introduced by the USDA. This nutrition guide grouped food in 7 main groups which would help the citizens to maintain a nutritive diet with a reasonable price during World War II, The Basic 7 food groups were:

  1. Green and yellow vegetables;
  2. Oranges, tomatoes, grapefruit;
  3. Potatoes and other vegetables and fruits;
  4. Milk and milk products;
  5. Meat, poultry, fish, or eggs;
  6. Bread, flour, and cereals;
  7. Butter and fortified margarine.

1945

The Food and Agricultural Organization of United Nations (FAO) was founded. The FAO has outlined the top priorities in its fight against the hunger:

  • Help eliminate hunger, food insecurity and malnutrition;
  • Make agriculture, forestry, and fisheries more productive and sustainable;
  • Reduce rural poverty;
  • Enable inclusive and efficient agricultural and food systems;
  • Increase the resilience of livelihoods to threats and crises.

1956

The Basic 7 are not longer supported and the United States Department of Agriculture recommended its "Basic Four" food groups.

This approach was recommended and mantained for over 40 years in the United States.

Basic Four groups were:

  1. Milk
  2. Meat
  3. Fruit and Vegetables
  4. Bread and Cereals.

1972

The Sweden's National Board of Health and Welfare saw forced to develop the idea of "basic foods" that were both cheap and nutritious. This pressure was due to the high food prices in the country back. The suggestion was a triangle in which the basic foods were at the base.

The idea was converted into the first food pyramid, which was introduced to the public in 1974. The pyramid was divided into basic foods at the base, including milk, cheese, margarine, bread, cereals and potato;

A large section of supplemental vegetables and fruit; and an apex of supplemental meat, fish and egg.

2000

The food-based dietary guidelines started being adopted by several countries. Around 70 nations had their own guide at the end of the decade. However, by the end of 2007 and mid of 2008, the food prices skyrocket and FAO's countries members started questioning the role of the Organization to control the rising in prices.

The food prices plunged due to the 2008's financial crisis, but reached new highs by the end of 2012.

2010

The Barilla Center for Food & Nutrition (BCFN) developed the Double Pyramid. A sustainable approach which combines the traditional food pyramid and a reversed one. The latter has the base upside down which represents the environmental impact that each food group from its production to the final consumer.

A recent study published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Science reveals that, in the United States, beef production requires 28 times more land to produce than pork or chicken, 11 times more water, and creates five times more greenhouse gas emissions.

A large section of supplemental vegetables and fruit; and an apex of supplemental meat, fish and egg.

2011

The USDA Center for Nutrition Policy and Promotion published a new diagram for Americans. It is a simple circle divided into five groups: Vegetables, Fruits, Protein, Grains and Dairy.

This was the introduction to the MyPlate, which replaced the previous representation (MyPyramid) which was considered confusing for many consumers and it was not well accepted. This new diagram ended 19 years of food pyramid iconography in the country.

...

The actual concern regarding to Dietary Guidelines is its relationship with sustainability. Not only nutrition and environment, but also the economic and socio-cultural impacts. This approach has been increasingly debated and countries have started to incorporate sustainability in their food polices and education programs.

The new recommendation is to motive the countries to recommend seasonal and local foods, the reduction of waste, red and processed meat, as well as highly processed foods and added-sugar-beverages.

A comprehensive publication of FAO and the Food Climate Research Network, which details how countries are dealing with this subject can be found here.
